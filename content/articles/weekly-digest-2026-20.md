The week's economic signal ran through energy. An inflation print that reran hotter than expected, an Iran-linked Strait of Hormuz closure driving oil prices higher, and the knock-on effects for monetary policy, emerging market vulnerability, and corporate cost structures formed the backbone of the week. Alongside that, U.S.-China trade negotiations showed up as a series of gestures rather than a deal, while corporate and legal cross-currents — a Samsung workers' strike, the Singapore Airlines–Air India integration drag, an SEC settlement with Adani, and a SCOTUS ruling on trucking broker liability — added sector-level texture. These stories are connected: energy supply shocks raise input costs across the board, force central banks to hold rates higher for longer, and compress margins in energy-intensive sectors from aviation to semiconductors.

## Energy price shock and inflation re-anchoring

April U.S. consumer inflation came in above forecasts, and May headline inflation is expected to breach 4% for the first time since mid-2025. The driver is partly cost-push: the Iran war and Hormuz closure pushed crude higher, lifting energy input costs across the supply chain. The AS–AD mechanism is straightforward — an adverse supply shock shifts the AS curve left, raising the price level for any given level of output. What makes this different from the 2021–22 episode is the starting point: inflation expectations had only recently re-anchored near the Fed's 2% target, and the bond market had been pricing rate cuts for most of 2026. The yield sell-off that followed — 10-year Treasury yields hit a one-year high — reflects repricing of the expected rate path: markets now expect the Fed to hold longer, with the term premium rising as oil-driven inflation uncertainty increases duration risk.

India illustrates the pass-through mechanics vividly. The country imports roughly 90% of its oil and raised fuel prices in response to the same energy shock. For a net energy importer with low short-run substitution capacity, a terms-of-trade shock of this kind forces a real income loss. The macro transmission runs through the current account (deteriorating oil import bill → rupee depreciation → imported inflation feedback) and the fiscal account (fuel subsidy drag or targeted cash transfers as policy responses). The distributional incidence is regressive: lower-income households spend a larger share of income on energy and food, and have lower elasticity of substitution.

## U.S.–China trade: gestures without a deal

The week's U.S.-China interaction produced announcements but not agreements. Trump said Xi had agreed to buy Boeing jets and soybeans; nothing was put in writing. Nvidia's H200 chips were cleared for China sale; Citigroup and Goldman Sachs executives met Beijing counterparts. The pattern — approvals and half-signals issued as individual data points rather than part of a structured framework — creates a specific economic problem. Trade policy uncertainty acts as an investment tax on firms with long planning horizons: when the direction of tariff policy is unknowable, firms delay capital expenditure and front-run supply chain adjustments. The short-run dynamic tends to produce an import surge ahead of prospective tariff hikes, followed by a collapse. Medium-run supply chain reorientation (reshoring, third-country diversification) crystallises slowly and is costly.

Argentina's addition of two mining projects to its Large Investment Incentive Regime (RIGI) — ~US$2.1 billion in committed investment — is a counterpoint: a country offering regulatory stability to attract capital inflows in a world where policy unpredictability is the norm. RIGI functions as a country-risk reduction mechanism, shifting the capital account toward inflow by guaranteeing tax and regulatory stability.

## Corporate and legal cross-currents

Samsung workers' strike over pay parity carries market-level stakes. Memory chips are a concentrated oligopoly — Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron control the vast majority of global DRAM and NAND supply. A four-plus-week Samsung strike creates a supply-side shock that lifts memory prices as buyers shift orders to competitors, raising input costs for electronics manufacturers and data centre operators. The welfare balance is not clean: value shifts from Samsung shareholders to workers, and consumer losses from higher memory prices are partly offset by the income transfer to labor. The pass-through is fast because substitute supply is limited in the short run.

Singapore Airlines posted record FY2026 revenue (SG$20.5 billion) while Air India dragged the combined entity with a SG$3.56 billion loss — the conglomerate discount visible in the divergence. The Air India integration is in the early destruction phase: restructuring costs and route rationalisation compress margins before synergy benefits materialise. Three to seven years is the typical airline turnaround horizon; Etihad-Alitalia is the cautionary benchmark. Also worth noting: a SCOTUS ruling that federal law does not preempt state lawsuits against trucking brokers for negligent hiring (CH Robinson) expands tort liability in a fragmented, low-margin industry; the SEC's US$6 million settlement with Gautam Adani signals continued enforcement posture despite the size being small relative to the alleged harm.

## Looking ahead

The next data point to watch is the May CPI print. If headline inflation stays above 4%, the bond sell-off extends and rate cut expectations compress further — with direct implications for mortgage rates, corporate refinancing costs, and emerging market capital flows. On trade, any resumption of tariff escalation would front-run again into import demand. And on energy, the Hormuz situation remains the swing factor: a de-escalation would unwind a meaningful portion of this week's inflation premium.
