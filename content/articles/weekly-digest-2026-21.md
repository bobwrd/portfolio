The Fed moved toward limited payment accounts for fintechs, India pushed Apple deeper into an antitrust case, and the Supreme Court kept mifepristone moving by telehealth and mail. None of those stories is about the same market, but they share one pattern: the gate, not the sticker price, is where power sits.

## Money still runs on permission

The Fed’s limited payment accounts proposal looks like inclusion until you read the fine print: access to rails without intraday credit, the discount window, or interest on reserves is not the same as access to a bank balance sheet. That is why the real fight is not about apps. It is about who can move money cheaply, who must pre-fund, and who absorbs the loss when something goes wrong.

That same split shows up in the week’s other finance stories. Cambodia’s microfinance debate and Thailand’s credit gaps both point to a simple problem: cleaner balance sheets often mean tighter credit at the edge. A lender can look healthier while low-income borrowers get pushed into short-term, expensive products or out of the system entirely. First-loss guarantees, like the Africa proposals floating around Macron’s push, help at the margin. They do not replace debt relief or real risk sharing.

## Platforms charge twice

Apple’s antitrust fight in India, the FTC pressure on food-delivery pricing, and Amazon’s battle over French book-delivery fees all sit on the same logic. The platform does not just set the price of the product. It also sets the fee around the product: ranking, delivery, default settings, cancellation friction, commission, and access to the channel itself.

That is why these cases matter even when the headline numbers are small. A 30% app-store take rate or a delivery fee floor changes who can compete, who can survive, and which users end up paying more than they expected. Once the gatekeeper can charge for access, posted prices stop telling the full story.

## Courts decide who can wait

Access to justice is often treated as a rights issue. This week showed it is also a timing issue. The Purdue opioid claims have already produced more than $57 billion in settlement talk, yet victims still ran into paperwork and filing hurdles years later. Ohio justices were uneasy about records rules that make public access harder than it should be. The Supreme Court’s mifepristone stay kept the status quo, but it also showed how quickly a right can turn into a logistics problem when courts and agencies move in different directions.

Procedure is a price. If you have money, time, and counsel, you can pay it. If you do not, delay itself becomes the barrier. That is why legal access is never only about the final ruling. It is about records, filings, travel, and the hours needed to keep a claim alive.

## AI is becoming a staffing plan

The AI story this week was not a flashy product launch. It was capital intensity and labor reordering. Anthropic’s projected revenue, paired with a $1.25 billion-a-month compute deal, says the model business is now tied to power, chips, and long contracts. Google, Workday, and JPMorgan all pointed in the same direction: more AI work, fewer pure task-execution roles, and more pressure on teams to justify headcount.

That does not mean every job disappears. It means the mix changes. Some firms will use AI to do more with the same staff. Others will use it to freeze hiring and shift risk onto the people left inside the org chart. The real question is who keeps the surplus when output rises: workers, managers, or the platform that owns the model and the interface.

Next week, watch three things: whether the Fed turns its limited-account idea into a firmer rule, whether Apple-style gatekeeping keeps drawing legal fire, and whether AI hiring shows up as slower headcount growth instead of just more pilot projects. If those lines move together, access will keep getting more expensive even when markets look calm.
