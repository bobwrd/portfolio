I have been working through the Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS) — India's primary household survey for employment statistics — and a few patterns are worth flagging before I turn them into something more formal.

## The informal sector does not shrink

The dominant narrative of economic development holds that formalisation is the natural trajectory: as economies grow, workers move from agriculture to industry, and from informal work to formal employment with contracts, social protection, and legal status.

Indian data complicates this. The share of workers in informal employment has remained persistently high even as GDP has grown. The manufacturing sector, which theoretically absorbs labour moving out of agriculture, has not expanded formal employment at the rate models predict. A significant portion of India's industrial growth is happening in small, unregistered enterprises — firms that stay below the regulatory thresholds that trigger formal obligations.

This matters because the informal sector is not a transitional state on the way to something better for most workers. For a large proportion, it is the stable endpoint.

## Gender gaps are not uniform

The aggregate gender participation gap in Indian employment is widely reported. Less discussed is the regional and sectoral variation underneath it.

In some states, female labour force participation has risen meaningfully over the decade. In others, it has barely moved. The variation correlates with some expected factors — education levels, urbanisation — but also with factors that are harder to quantify: social norms, caste, the extent to which women's paid work is seen as a marker of household status failure versus economic necessity.

The policy implication is that a national intervention designed around an average gap will miss most of what is actually happening. The distribution matters more than the mean.

## What I am still uncertain about

The PLFS methodology changed in 2017-18, which creates breaks in time-series comparisons. Adjusting for those breaks is non-trivial, and I am not yet confident in my approach. I am also uncertain about how much of the informal sector persistence is measurement (firms that exist but are not captured) versus a genuine structural feature of the economy.

More when I have cleaner results.
