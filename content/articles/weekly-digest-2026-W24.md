U.S. inflation stayed hot at 4.2% in May, and the week’s other big stories fit the same pattern: access got easier to request, but the bill moved into hidden layers.[^1] Fintechs got closer to Fed rails, subscriptions spread across banking and apps, and AI kept pulling in more capital. The surface got smoother. The price did not go away.

## Finance without a shock absorber

The Fed’s proposed limited payment accounts are a good example. Fintechs could move money across Fed rails, but they would still be missing intraday credit, the discount window, and interest on reserves.[^2] That is plumbing without much protection. It helps if the problem is speed. It does less if the problem is a cash-flow shock, a failed transfer, or a bad week for liquidity.

ING’s move to a subscription banking model points the same way.[^3] Banks and fintechs are no longer just selling access. They are packaging access into tiers, fees, and product bundles. That can make the front door wider, but it also means the user pays in smaller, less visible pieces. Inclusion is not just about opening an account. It is about whether the account can absorb stress without pushing the cost back onto the customer.

## The sticker price is the wrong price

Platform markets keep proving that the listed price is only part of the price. Apple said the App Store handled more than $1.4 trillion in billings and sales in 2025, while also adding new subscription bundle tools that make it easier to keep purchases inside one gatekeeper.[^4] At the same time, French book-delivery minimums survived a legal challenge from Amazon, and food and grocery delivery apps kept loading checkout with service fees, delivery fees, and other add-ons.[^5][^6]

That is where everyday microeconomics gets real. A user sees a meal price, a book price, or a subscription price. Then the platform layers on the route cost, the convenience fee, the bundle, and the commission. The result is not just a higher bill. It is a different decision. People buy less, switch less, or stay inside the same platform because leaving is harder than it looks.

## Justice by delay

The legal system is still sorting people by time, distance, and paperwork. California legal nonprofits warned that court-reporter shortages can wipe out the verbatim record for people who cannot afford their own reporter, and Hawaiʻi set up a task force after a lawyer shortage hit rural and neighbor-island communities hard.[^7][^8] Those are not side issues. They are the economics of whether a claim can survive long enough to matter.

The same logic shows up in immigration and enforcement. The Justice Department moved to strip citizenship from 17 naturalized people, while the House passed a $70 billion immigration-enforcement bill.[^9][^10] That may read like policy, but the cost lands on the person who has to find counsel, gather records, and wait. Delay is not neutral. It favors whoever can keep paying.

## AI’s bill is the capex

AI is starting to look less like software and more like a financing chain. OpenAI and Anthropic both moved toward public markets, Morgan Stanley said AI-related debt issuance could reach nearly $570 billion in 2026, and the largest cloud firms are still spending at giant scale on compute and infrastructure.[^11][^12] That is the real bottleneck: chips, power, land, cooling, and the money to pay for all of it.

That matters for labor too. CNBC’s report on a compute wall is the right frame here. If compute gets tighter, firms will not automate everything. They will automate the pieces that fit the budget, then keep people in the loop for the rest.[^13] The gains will not land evenly. The firm that owns the data, workflow, and hardware gets the first claim on surplus. The rest of the market gets a better interface and a larger bill.

## What to watch next week

Watch whether regulators keep pushing on payment access without real risk sharing, whether platform fee fights move from the courtroom into product design, and whether AI spending starts to hit harder limits in power and capital markets. The week showed the same thing in finance, courts, and tech: access is widening, but the toll is being moved into the queue.
