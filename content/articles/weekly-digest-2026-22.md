The week’s cleanest signal was that access got cheaper to start and harder to finish. Bloomberg Law said AI-generated pro se filings are flooding dockets, with non-prisoner pro se civil cases rising from 11% to 16.8% over five years. AP also reported that San Francisco’s main immigration court shut down after judges were fired, retired, or resigned, pushing roughly 117,000 cases to Concord.[^1][^2] Those are not separate stories. They are the same one: software and restructuring can open the front door, but the real cost shows up in the queue.

## Filing is not the same as justice

A person who cannot afford counsel can now draft and file faster than before. But the court still has to sort the mess. Clerks, judges, and opposing lawyers absorb more review work, and the extra friction lands hardest on people already short on time and money. In immigration court, a longer trip and a larger backlog work like a hidden filing fee. If you have resources, you buy patience. If you do not, the clock becomes part of the punishment.[^1][^2]

That is why access to justice keeps looking like an economics problem, not just a legal one. The filing fee is visible. The travel time, the delay, the lost workday, and the need for a lawyer to clean up bad paperwork are the real costs. AI can lower the first one and raise the last three.

## Inclusion is moving down to the rail layer

The Fed’s proposal for limited payment accounts for fintechs would let more nonbanks touch central-bank rails, but without intraday credit, the discount window, or interest on balances. That is broader reach, not full membership. China is showing the other side of the same problem: the PBOC told banks to boost lending because credit weakness persists, which is a reminder that access to an account does not mean cheap liquidity when households and firms are squeezed.[^3][^4]

The development version of this story is similar. African leaders pushed for a rethink in how risk is priced, while analysts said first-loss guarantees were not enough without debt relief or payment pauses. A nicer financial wrapper does not help much if the borrower still carries the downside alone. Inclusion becomes real only when the terms match the cash flow.[^5]

## Platform pricing is still a hidden fee stack

Consumer Reports said delivery apps can add 48% to 52% on top of food and taxes, and asked the FTC for all-in pricing and an end to subscription traps. France’s top court also rejected Amazon’s challenge to minimum delivery fees on books, while Indonesia cut the maximum ride-hailing commission to 8%.[^6][^7][^8] Different markets, same logic. The platform sits between buyer and seller, then decides how much of the spread it keeps.

The screen looks simple. The pricing is not. Once a platform controls routing, ranking, delivery, or access to the channel itself, the posted price stops telling you much about what the transaction really costs. The fee is no longer beside the product. It is built into it.

## AI is becoming a control system, not just a tool

Robinhood’s move to let customers use AI agents for trading and credit-card purchases makes that plain. Reuters said only 21% of leaders in a Deloitte survey thought their organizations had mature governance for agentic AI.[^9] That gap matters more than the demo. If software can act, the real issue is who can reverse the action, who can explain it, and who bears the loss when it goes wrong.

The same shift is showing up inside firms too. AI is less about one-off drafting and more about who owns the workflow, the audit trail, and the headcount plan. That is why the labor story is not just “jobs lost” or “jobs saved.” It is which tasks move to machines, which move to review work, and which people get left with more responsibility and less leverage.

Next week, watch whether courts keep treating AI filings as a process problem, whether regulators push harder on all-in pricing, and whether central-bank access keeps expanding without the safety net that makes liquidity usable. If those three moves keep lining up, the week was not a one-off. It was the direction of travel.
