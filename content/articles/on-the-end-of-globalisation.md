For decades, the prevailing assumption was simple: open borders for goods, capital, and people produce prosperity. Comparative advantage meant countries specialised; supply chains stretched across continents; prices fell. The Washington Consensus packaged this into policy.

That consensus is cracking — not because economists were wrong about the mechanics, but because the politics always ran on a timer.

## What changed

The 2008 financial crisis was the first visible fracture. Recovery was uneven: aggregate GDP bounced back in rich countries; median wages did not. The gap between who benefited from trade and who absorbed its costs became legible to voters in a way that trade models struggle to capture.

Then came COVID-19. "Just-in-time" supply chains, optimised for cost, turned out to be fragile. Semiconductor shortages shut down car factories in Germany because chips were made in Taiwan. Pharmaceutical supply chains ran through single points of failure. Efficiency and resilience, it turned out, are not the same objective.

Now add the US-China technology decoupling, export controls on advanced semiconductors, and "friendshoring" — the deliberate reshaping of supply chains along geopolitical lines. Globalisation is not ending; it is sorting itself into blocs.

## What this means for Singapore

Singapore is one of the most trade-dependent economies in the world. Total trade is roughly three times GDP. The city-state built its prosperity on being a neutral node: a port, a financial centre, a logistics hub that served everyone.

Neutrality is harder to maintain when the world bifurcates. The pressure to pick sides — or at least to not be seen helping the wrong side — increases. The Electronics sector, which accounts for a significant share of Singapore's exports, sits at the intersection of US chip controls and Chinese manufacturing.

The government's response has been pragmatic: diversify trade partners, invest in domestic capabilities for critical industries, and maintain the diplomatic capital to keep talking to both Washington and Beijing. Whether this is enough depends on how sharp the bifurcation gets.

## The harder question

The deeper issue is distributional. Even if aggregate trade gains are real, they are not automatic. Countries that manage the transition well — with active labour-market policy, education systems that retrain rather than just credential, and fiscal capacity to buffer shocks — will capture more of the upside. Countries that do not will find that the gains go to capital while the losses land on workers.

Globalisation did not fail because the economics were wrong. It ran into trouble because the politics of distributing its benefits were neglected for too long. The countries that figure out the politics second time around will do better.
