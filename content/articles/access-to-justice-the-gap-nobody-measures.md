When we talk about the rule of law, we usually mean two things: that laws are predictably applied, and that no one is above them. Both are important. But they assume something prior — that people can actually access the legal system when they need it.

That prior assumption is badly underexamined.

## What "access" actually means

Legal access has layers. At the most basic level: can you find out what your rights are? Legal language is not plain language. A lease, an employment contract, or a debt agreement is often incomprehensible to the person signing it. If you do not know you have a right, you cannot enforce it.

The next layer is representation. In most adversarial legal systems, the quality of your outcome depends significantly on the quality of your legal representation. Legal aid exists in many jurisdictions, but it is chronically underfunded, narrowly scoped, and frequently unavailable for civil matters — the very disputes that affect ordinary people most: landlord-tenant disputes, employment claims, family law.

The final layer is time and money. Even when representation is available, litigation is slow and expensive. The cost of going to court is sometimes higher than the value of what you are claiming. Rational actors give up. That rational giving-up is invisible in outcome statistics.

## The measurement problem

Court systems report case outcomes: conviction rates, civil judgment values, appeal rates. They rarely report the universe of people who had a legitimate legal claim and did not pursue it. The gap between the population with legal needs and the population that actually reaches court is large, poorly documented, and almost never addressed by reform efforts.

This is not accidental. Institutions tend to measure what they can control. A court can tell you how long its cases take to resolve. It cannot easily tell you how many people looked at the cost of filing and walked away.

The result is that accountability focuses on process (how quickly did cases move?) rather than access (who could not get through the door?). Reforms optimise for the measured variable and leave the unmeasured problem in place.

## CivicAid and the literacy gap

One part of the access problem — legal literacy — is tractable. If people understand basic laws that apply to their daily lives, they are better placed to protect their rights before disputes escalate, negotiate from an informed position, and know when they need professional help.

That is the premise behind CivicAid, an app I am building to make key Singaporean laws accessible to ordinary people. It is a small intervention in a large problem. But making legal information clear, accurate, and easy to navigate is a prerequisite for everything else. You cannot exercise rights you do not know you have.

The larger problem — structural underfunding of legal aid, court costs, the time burden of litigation — requires policy, not apps. But measuring the access gap honestly is where that policy has to start.
