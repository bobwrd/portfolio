Brazil’s subsidized motorcycle loan for delivery drivers looked like a clean inclusion policy, but it only reached workers with at least six months on a platform and 100 completed trips or deliveries.[^1] Spain’s migrant regularisation drive pulled in about 900,000 applications, roughly double what officials expected.[^2] Different systems, same shape: access is still widening, but only after people prove history, wait in line, and absorb the cost of being legible.

## Access now comes with proof

The same thing is happening in courts. Britain’s employment tribunal backlog reached 531,000 cases at the start of 2026, after more than 64,000 new claims in the first quarter.[^3] In Hawaiʻi, a new task force is looking at a lawyer shortage that leaves rural and neighbor-island residents with thinner access to representation.[^4] A right that depends on delay is not evenly available. If you have savings, transport, and time off work, you can keep a claim alive. If you do not, the system quietly prices you out without ever saying no.

That is why “access” is too simple a word. A program can widen the front door and still leave the same people stuck at the gate. The hidden cost is often not a filing fee. It is the paperwork, the travel, the missed shift, and the weeks spent waiting for a slot that may never come.

## The sticker price hides the route fee

Everyday pricing kept pushing in the same direction. Consumer Reports found that Uber and Lyft charged different prices for the same ride at roughly the same time, with some identical routes varying by as much as 163%.[^5] Apple said the App Store handled more than $1.4 trillion in billings and sales in 2025, while 90% of that activity did not pay Apple a commission.[^6] Those numbers point to the same thing: the platform’s power is not only the posted fee. It is the route through which demand reaches supply.

That is why subscriptions, bundles, and queue-jumping products work so well. They move the cost away from the headline price and into convenience, timing, or cancellation friction. A user thinks they are buying a ride, a delivery, or an app. What they are often buying is permission to use a lane the platform controls.

## AI is becoming a capital and permission business

AI is following the same pattern, only at a larger scale. Morgan Stanley expects AI-related debt issuance to reach nearly $570 billion in 2026, while hyperscalers are still expected to spend about $700 billion this year.[^7] OpenAI reportedly burned $3.7 billion in the first quarter of 2026 on $5.7 billion of revenue.[^8] The race is no longer about model demos alone. It is about who can finance chips, power, land, cooling, and the time needed to turn compute into control.

That is also why the policy fights are moving outward. U.S. bank regulators are asking lenders to map how they use AI in lending, KYC, sanctions screening, and vendor controls.[^9] The Trump administration backed xAI in a lawsuit brought by the NAACP over emissions from data centers powering its AI system.[^10] The question is no longer whether AI exists. It is who owns the workflow, who pays for the infrastructure, and who absorbs the harm when the machine needs a bigger footprint.

## What to watch next week

Watch whether court backlogs keep deepening, whether platform pricing fights keep moving from the sticker to the route, and whether AI spending starts to meet harder financing limits. This week’s pattern was blunt: the door is still open, but the queue is doing more of the work.
