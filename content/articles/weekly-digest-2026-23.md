The week's clearest pattern was not that anything became cheaper. It was that access got easier to request and harder to finish. The Justice Department is trying to cut a 3.7 million-case immigration backlog, and the Fed is opening payment rails to fintechs without the full bank backstop.[^1][^2] Both moves widen the front door. Both also push more of the cost into the queue.

## Courts are charging by distance

Immigration courts show the basic math. AP reported that San Francisco's immigration court was thrown into chaos after judges were fired, retired, or resigned, and that roughly 117,000 cases were shifted to Concord, about 30 miles away.[^3] That looks procedural. It behaves like a price increase. Travel time, lost wages, childcare, and missed hearings are not abstract frictions. They are the bill. For people with cash, delay is annoying. For everyone else, delay is the system's way of deciding who can keep a claim alive.

AI filing tools do not fix that. Bloomberg Law reported that AI-generated pro se filings are flooding dockets, with non-prisoner pro se civil cases rising from 11% to 16.8% over five years.[^4] Faster filing can help people get in the door. It also shifts cleanup work onto clerks, judges, and the other side. The system gets more open at entry and more expensive in processing.

## Inclusion without a backstop

The Fed's limited payment accounts proposal is the same pattern in finance. It gives fintechs access to Fed rails, but not intraday credit, the discount window, or interest on reserves.[^2] That is reach without full membership. It helps money move. It does not tell you who absorbs the shock when a platform stumbles or liquidity tightens.

That distinction matters because inclusion is often measured too shallowly. An account is not the same as resilience. A payment rail is not the same as a balance sheet. When the missing piece is the safety net, the user still carries the downside. The convenience is real. So is the fragility.

## The app layer keeps its tolls

The same logic shows up in platform markets. Apple said the App Store handled $1.4 trillion in billings and sales in 2025, with 90% of transactions outside commission, but the commission-bearing slice still runs through Apple's fee structure.[^5] Reuters reported that the Supreme Court left in place an order holding Apple in contempt in the Epic fight, while France's top court rejected Amazon's challenge over minimum book-delivery fees.[^6][^7] Different products, same setup. The platform controls the route to the customer, then charges for the route.

Labor is pushing back. Massachusetts ride-share drivers formed the first certified U.S. union for Uber and Lyft drivers, covering nearly 70,000 contractors, and the ILO has started final talks on binding standards for platform workers.[^8][^9] Those moves are a sign that workers are no longer treating the app as neutral. They are treating it as the place where pay, access, and control are set.

## AI is moving into workflow ownership

The AI story this week was less about flashy demos than about control. Reuters reported that law firms are building AI twins of partners, Kirkland & Ellis plans to spend $500 million on its own AI platform, and Epsilon India says AI has let it do far more work with roughly the same headcount.[^10][^11][^12] That points to where the surplus is going: to the firm that owns the workflow, the data, and the review step.

Canada's AI strategy fits the same pattern. It promises growth and job creation, but the gains will depend on which firms can turn model access into process control.[^13] The model is not the whole story. The real asset is the system around it.

Next week, watch whether courts keep treating backlog as a scheduling problem, whether the Fed moves beyond narrow access and into real risk sharing, and whether platform and AI rules start to bite at the fee, routing, and review layers. That is where the cost has moved.
